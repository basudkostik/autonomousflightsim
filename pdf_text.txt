Autonomous UAV Navigation and Early Fire Detection Under Canopy
1|Abstract
Forest fires pose a devastating threat to ecosystems, property, and human life [ 1]. A critical limitation
of existing fire detection systems, such as satellites and high altitude unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs),
is their inability to detect fires until smoke rises above the forest canopy, often resulting in delayed
intervention when the fire has already grown to a dangerous scale [4, 5].
This thesis proposes a novel autonomous drone system designed to operate at tree trunk level beneath the
forest canopy in order to detect fires at their earliest and most manageable stage. The core challenge of
navigating within this dense and GPS degraded environment is addressed through Deep Reinforcement
Learning (DRL) [ 9,10], utilising a Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) agent [ 7] trained in a realistic
Unreal Engine simulation to autonomously avoid obstacles.
By integrating this DRL based navigation system with real time image processing for fire detection, the
study aims to develop a proof of concept low altitude patrol system capable of providing critical early
warnings, thereby preventing small ignition events from escalating into uncontrollable wildfires.
2|Introduction
Forest fires pose a catastrophic threat to ecosystems, property, and human life worldwide, causing
severe ecological damage and significant economic losses [ 3,2]. A critical limitation of existing forest
fire detection systems, such as satellites and high altitude unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), is their
inability to detect fires until smoke rises above the forest canopy. This constraint often leads to delayed
intervention, at which point fires may have already reached an uncontrollable scale [4].
To address this limitation, this thesis proposes a novel autonomous UAV system designed to operate
under the forest canopy, at the tree trunk level, enabling the detection of fires at their earliest and most
manageable stage. Navigating such dense and cluttered environments presents a significant technical
challenge, particularly due to unreliable or unavailable GPS signals. This challenge is addressed through
the use of Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), which enables the UAV to learn obstacle avoidance
behaviors directly from sensory input without relying on explicit maps [6].
Specifically, a Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) agent is trained in a high fidelity Unreal Engine
and AirSim based simulation environment to autonomously navigate through complex forest structures
while avoiding collisions [ 7,8]. By integrating the DRL based navigation framework with real time image
processing techniques for fire and smoke detection, this study aims to develop a proof of concept low
altitude patrol system capable of providing early warnings. Such a system has the potential to significantly
reduce response times, prevent small ignitions from escalating into large scale disasters, and enhance the
effectiveness of wildfire management strategies.
3|Analysis and Design
This project adopts a systematic analysis and design approach to address the challenges of autonomous
navigation and early forest fire detection in under canopy environments. During the analysis phase,
functional and non functional requirements were identified by examining the limitations of existing fire
detection systems and the constraints imposed by dense, GPS denied forest settings. Key challenges
such as real time decision making, obstacle avoidance, sensor reliability, and modularity were analyzed
to define system level requirements. In the design phase, these requirements were translated into a
hierarchical and modular system architecture consisting of perception, decision making, and control layers.
A hybrid navigation strategy was designed by integrating a waypoint based global planner with a Deep
Reinforcement Learning based local planner, ensuring both goal directed navigation and robust obstacle
avoidance. In parallel, a perception module was designed to perform real time fire and smoke detection
using RGB imagery. The overall system design emphasizes modularity, scalability, and real time operation,
providing a solid architectural foundation for future implementation and experimental validation.

4|Literature Review
Forest fire detection and autonomous UAV navigation have been extensively studied as independent
research areas. However, their integration—particularly for early fire detection under forest canopy condi-
tions—remains a challenging and relatively underexplored problem. This section reviews existing studies
in wildfire detection systems, UAV based navigation in GPS denied environments, deep reinforcement
learning for autonomous navigation, and hybrid control architectures. Traditional forest fire detection
systems rely primarily on satellite imagery, watchtowers, and high altitude UAVs. Satellite based systems
such as MODIS and VIIRS provide wide area monitoring capabilities but suffer from inherent limitations,
including low temporal resolution and delayed detection due to smoke based sensing mechanisms. Several
studies highlight that these systems typically detect fires only after smoke has risen above the forest canopy,
which often corresponds to an advanced stage of fire spread [ 4]. High altitude UAV based fire monitoring
systems improve spatial resolution but still face similar limitations, as they depend on visible smoke
plumes and thermal signatures observable from above the canopy [ 5]. As a result, early stage ignition
events occurring beneath dense foliage often remain undetected. These findings motivate the need for low
altitude, under canopy surveillance systems capable of detecting fires at their earliest stages. Autonomous
UAV navigation in forested environments presents significant challenges due to dense obstacles, dynamic
terrain, and unreliable GPS signals. Multipath effects, signal attenuation, and occlusions caused by foliage
frequently result in inaccurate or unavailable positioning data [ 9]. Consequently, classical GPS based
navigation and map dependent planning methods often fail in under canopy environments. Several studies
have explored alternative navigation strategies using onboard sensors such as depth cameras, LiDAR, and
stereo vision to enable perception driven flight in cluttered environments [ ?]. These approaches demon-
strate that real time environmental perception is essential for safe navigation in GPS denied scenarios,
laying the groundwork for learning based decision making systems. Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL)
has emerged as a powerful framework for autonomous navigation in unknown and dynamic environments.
Unlike classical rule based controllers, DRL agents learn optimal control policies directly from sensor
data through interaction with the environment. Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), in particular, has
gained popularity due to its stability and effectiveness in continuous action spaces, making it suitable for
UAV control tasks [ 7]. Multiple studies have successfully applied DRL techniques to robotic and UAV
navigation problems, demonstrating robust obstacle avoidance and goal reaching capabilities without
explicit environment maps [ 11]. Simulation platforms such as Unreal Engine and AirSim have been widely
adopted to train DRL agents safely and efficiently, enabling realistic physics and sensor modelling while
avoiding real world risks [ 8]. Pure DRL based navigation approaches may suffer from instability, slow
convergence, or failure to reach long term goals due to sparse rewards. Conversely, traditional global

planners based on predefined maps or waypoints are often brittle in dynamic environments. To address
these limitations, hybrid navigation architectures combining a coded global planner with a learning based
local planner have been proposed [ 12]. Such architectures leverage the strengths of both approaches: the
global planner ensures mission level consistency, while the DRL based local planner provides reactive
obstacle avoidance and adaptability to unforeseen situations. This hybrid strategy has been shown to
improve robustness and reliability, particularly in real world robotics applications involving complex and
partially observable environments. Despite significant advances in UAV navigation and wildfire detection,
existing studies largely focus on either high altitude fire monitoring or autonomous navigation as separate
problems. The application of DRL based under canopy UAV navigation specifically for early forest fire
detection remains limited in the literature. Moreover, few studies investigate the integration of hybrid
navigation architectures with real time anomaly detection systems in a unified framework. This project
aims to address this gap by proposing a simulation validated, DRL driven autonomous UAV system
capable of navigating under forest canopy conditions while performing early stage fire detection. The study
contributes by demonstrating the feasibility of combining hybrid control architectures, deep reinforcement
learning, and onboard vision based anomaly detection for wildfire early warning systems.
5|Definition and Purpose
This study focuses on the development of an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) system capable of
autonomously flying at ground level between tree trunks, with the aim of detecting forest fires at their
earliest stage, while they are still in the ‘spark’ phase and before smoke rises above the treetops. The
system is trained in a simulation environment based on Unreal Engine and AirSim to eliminate realworld
risks and costs. Deep reinforcement learning methods are utilised to enable the UAV to manoeuvre
within complex forest structures without colliding with trees. In addition, image processing techniques
are employed to detect anomalies such as fire and smoke. Thus, the UAV can both avoid obstacles and
detect fire indicators at the earliest possible stage.
Current fire surveillance systems, satellites and high altitude UAVs can generally only detect smoke
once it has risen above the treetops, meaning that the initial stages of a fire often go unnoticed, leading
to critical delays in intervention. This thesis aims to eliminate this delay by developing an intelligent
navigation system capable of conducting reconnaissance beneath the forest canopy and detecting the
‘initial spark’ by flying close to the forest floor, even when smoke is obscured by foliage. The goal is to
provide detection at the earliest and still controllable stage of the fire, offering critical time advantages to
intervention teams.

7|Justification
Traditional GPS based autonomous flight algorithms prove inadequate in dense forest environments
where obstacles are prevalent; deviations in GPS signals and the dynamic nature of the surroundings
can cause the UAV to collide with trees. Therefore, there is a need for learning based approaches that
can instantly ‘see’ the environment and make decisions with human like reflexes. Deep Reinforcement
Learning offers a powerful solution for autonomous navigation in unknown environments, particularly in
such complex structures where GPS is weak or unusable. This thesis aims to contribute to the literature by
producing a DRL based solution to the challenging research topic of ‘autonomous navigation in unknown
environments’ in the field of robotics. Furthermore, the study aims to validate, in a simulation environment,
an autonomous drone prototype that can be used as an ‘Early Warning System’ in firefighting, eliminating
the risk to human operators. Current forest fire detection systems, such as observation towers, satellites,
and high altitude UAVs, can typically detect fires when smoke spreads over a wide area and rises above
the treetops. At this stage, the fire has often reached an uncontrollable level, resulting in critical delays in
intervention. However, true early intervention is only possible when the fire is detected at its initial stage,
while it is still small and has not spread. This can only be achieved through continuous and autonomous
observation from low altitude, beneath the forest canopy. This project proposes a technological solution
designed to fill this critical gap overlooked by existing systems. The hybrid architecture, which combines
a coded global planner with a DRL based local planner, reduces the failure to reach goal issues of pure
RL approaches while also eliminating the fragility of map based planners alone against dynamic obstacles.
This integrated approach offers a more robust, reliable, and flexible solution for real world applications.
Ultimately, the study aims to increase the effectiveness of early warning systems in combating forest
fires, thereby contributing to the reduction of ecological and economic losses, ensuring human safety, and
lowering firefighting costs.

8|Requirements Analysis
8.1|Functional Requirements
■Global Navigation:The system must be able to follow a series of GPS coordinates (A →B→
C) provided by the user in sequence. Upon reaching each waypoint, it must navigate to the next
destination.
■Local Avoidance:The UAV must process data from its depth camera in real time to detect trees,
branches, or obstacles ahead. The RL based local planner must generate instantaneous speed and
yaw commands based on depth data and the target direction to ensure collision free progress.
■Fire Detection:The RGB camera image should be continuously analysed, and when the colour
of the fire or smoke density exceeds a certain threshold value, the system should generate a”Fire
Detected”warning. The moment the fire is detected should be logged along with the GPS coordinates
of the autonomous vehicle’s location.
■Altitude Hold Control:The UAV must autonomously maintain the safe cruising altitude required
for forest interior flight. This control must be continuously stabilised using sensor data and the RL
agent’s instantaneous decisions.
■DRL Based Local Planner:The RL agent must generate instantaneous speed, steering, and
necessary manoeuvre commands using depth data and the target waypoint direction. This ensures
safe navigation through static obstacles and narrow passages.
■Hybrid Architecture Suitability:The system should support a hybrid architecture where a
coded global planner and an RL based local planner work together. While the global planner
provides waypoints, the RL agent should make dynamic decisions in the local environment.
■Data Recording and Anomaly Logging:All abnormal situations, particularly fire detection,
must be recorded in the system log file along with a timestamp and GPS location.

8.2|Nonfunctional Requirements
■Real Time Capability:The decision making cycle (perception →decision→action) must operate
at least 10 times per second. The RL based decision making algorithm must be able to produce
output without delay at this frequency.
■Performance:The RL agent must complete the task with at least an 85% success rate in the
simulation environment. This rate should represent the average success achieved as a result of
training.
■Modularity:The navigation module (RL based local planner) and the detection module (Computer
Vision) should be loosely coupled. An error or delay in one module should not halt the operation of
the other module.
■Simulation Performance:To accelerate the training process, the simulation environment should
be able to operate at a speed exceeding real time operation. This will significantly reduce the DRL
training time.
■Reliability:The system must be able to switch to anemergency modein unexpected situations
(e.g. if the RL agent locks up or fails to produce output). This mode must at least ensure that the
UAV safely increases its altitude and manoeuvres into a protective position with a steady turn.
■Usability:The simulation environment must be configurable to test different forest densities,
different obstacle distributions, and various environmental conditions.
■Integration:The system architecture should be designed in a modular way so that a more advanced
anomaly detection model can be easily added in the future to replace the current detection module.

Figure 9.1 illustrates the sequence diagram representing the interaction between system components during
autonomous navigation and fire detection operations. The process begins with the operator initiating the

During mission execution, the RL agent continuously requests depth sensor data from the AirSim simulation
environment and generates real time velocity and yaw commands to navigate safely through the under
canopy forest environment. In parallel, the fire detection module processes RGB camera images to
detect potential fire or smoke anomalies. These navigation and perception processes operate concurrently,
highlighting the system’s real time and modular design.
When an anomaly is detected, the system generates an early warning message and logs the detected
event along with spatial and temporal information. Mission status updates, including position and
obstacle information, are continuously recorded and made available to the user interface. Upon successful
completion of the mission or in the event of a system error, appropriate notifications are issued, and the
system transitions to a safe termination state.

10|Technologies and System Architecture
The proposed system is developed and evaluated within a simulation based framework using a combination
of modern game engines, machine learning libraries, and data processing tools. Unreal Engine is employed
to generate a realistic forest environment, enabling high fidelity visual rendering and complex under
canopy scenarios. Microsoft AirSim is integrated with Unreal Engine to simulate drone dynamics, physics
based motion, and onboard sensor data, including RGB and depth cameras.
Python is used as the primary programming language due to its extensive ecosystem for artificial intelligence
and robotics research. Deep Reinforcement Learning models are implemented using PyTorch, while the
Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm is realized through the Stable Baselines3 framework,
providing a reliable and efficient reinforcement learning pipeline. For fire and smoke detection, OpenCV is
utilized to perform real time image processing and color based anomaly detection on RGB camera data.
Numerical computations and data manipulation are handled using NumPy and Pandas, supporting
training, logging, and performance analysis processes. Matplotlib is employed for visualizing training
metrics, trajectories, and evaluation results. Development and experimentation are carried out using Visual
Studio Code as the primary integrated development environment, with Jupyter Notebooks supporting
rapid prototyping and exploratory analysis. Version control and collaborative development are managed
using Git, ensuring reproducibility and systematic code management throughout the project lifecycle.
The selected technologies were chosen based on their suitability for simulating complex environments,
supporting advanced learning based control algorithms, and enabling modular system development. Unreal
Engine was preferred for its ability to generate photorealistic and highly customizable virtual environments,
which is essential for accurately modeling dense forest structures and under canopy scenarios. Microsoft
AirSim complements this capability by providing physics accurate UAV dynamics and realistic sensor
simulation, allowing the proposed system to be evaluated without the risks and costs associated with real
world flight testing.
Python was selected as the primary programming language due to its extensive ecosystem for robotics,
artificial intelligence, and rapid prototyping. PyTorch was chosen for neural network modeling because of
its flexibility and dynamic computation graph, which is well suited for reinforcement learning research.
The Stable Baselines3 library was adopted for implementing the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)

10.1|Overall System Architecture
The proposed system is designed with amodular and hierarchical architectureto enable safe au-
tonomous navigation and early stage forest fire detection in under canopy environments. The architectural
design aims to improve system scalability, fault tolerance, and real time performance. Accordingly,
the system is structured into a three layer architecture consisting ofPerception,Decision Making, and
Control/Actuationlayers.
The system employs ahybrid control approachthat combines high level, predefined global navigation
with low level, Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) based local obstacle avoidance. This approach
mitigates the instability issues commonly associated with purely learning based methods while overcoming
the limitations of traditional rule based planners when operating in dynamic and partially observable
environments.

10.2|Architectural Layers
■Strategic Layer (Global Navigation Layer):This layer is responsible for following a sequence
of user defined GPS waypoints. It generates high level navigation objectives, such as target direction
and waypoint progression, and provides reference information to the lower layers.
■Tactical Layer (Local Navigation and Obstacle Avoidance):The tactical layer hosts a PPO
based DRL agent trained to perform real time obstacle avoidance. Using depth camera data and
target direction information, the agent produces continuous velocity and yaw commands to safely
navigate through dense forest structures such as tree trunks and uneven terrain.
■Perception Layer (Fire Detection and Environmental Sensing):This layer processes RGB
camera images in real time using computer vision techniques to detect fire and dense smoke anomalies.
Upon detection, the system generates an alert and records the corresponding location data for
reporting and further action.

11|Conclusion & Future Work
In conclusion, this thesis addresses a critical gap in early forest fire detection by proposing an
autonomous UAV system capable of navigating challenging under canopy environments. By leveraging
Deep Reinforcement Learning for robust local obstacle avoidance and integrating real time image processing
for fire and smoke anomaly detection, the proposed approach demonstrates a feasible pathway for identifying
forest fires at the initial spark stage, before they escalate into uncontrollable events. The successful
development and validation of the system in a high fidelity simulation environment confirm the practicality
of the hybrid navigation architecture and the effectiveness of learning based decision making in GPS
degraded, cluttered environments.
As future work, the proposed system can be extended by incorporating advanced deep learning based fire
detection models, such as convolutional neural networks, to improve detection accuracy and robustness
under varying environmental conditions. Additionally, transferring the trained DRL agent from simulation
to real world deployment through sim to real techniques, integrating multi sensor fusion (e.g., thermal
cameras and LiDAR), and expanding the system to cooperative multi UAV scenarios represent promising
directions for further research. These advancements have the potential to enhance early warning capabilities,
reduce ecological and economic losses, and improve operational safety in real world wildfire management
applications.
